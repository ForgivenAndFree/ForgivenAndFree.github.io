# Porn Is Not Your Only Problem

> *"Two men went up into the temple to pray, one a Pharisee and the other a tax collector. The Pharisee, standing by himself, prayed thus: 'God, I thank you that I am not like other men, extortioners, unjust, adulterers, or even like this tax collector.'"*
> — Luke 18:10-11 (ESV)

---

## The Lie That Feels Like Humility

There is a particular deception that afflicts Christian men who struggle with pornography, and it is more dangerous than most because it disguises itself as brokenness.

It goes like this: "Porn is my great sin. It's the thing I wrestle with. If I could just get free from *this* — if I could finally conquer *this one thing* — I'd be walking with God the way I'm supposed to. I'd basically be the man God wants me to be."

Do you hear it? Do you hear the pride hiding inside the humility?

The man who says this is not confessing that he is a sinner. He is confessing that he has *a* sin — one big, dramatic, obvious sin — and implicitly declaring himself clean in every other department. He has looked at the Ten Commandments and decided that he is failing at one of them. Which means, in his own estimation, he is passing the other nine.

He is not. Neither are you. Neither am I. Neither is any human being who has ever lived, except for One.

---

## What the Catechism Actually Says

Let's take a walk through Luther's Small Catechism — not the commandment you already know you're breaking, but the ones you've been ignoring while all your spiritual attention was fixed on pornography.

### The First Commandment: "You shall have no other gods."

Luther explains: "We should fear, love, and trust in God above all things."

This is the commandment underneath all the others, and it is the one every human being breaks every day. Do you fear God above all things — above losing your job, your reputation, your health, your comfort? Do you love God above all things — above your children, your spouse, your plans, your leisure? Do you trust God above all things — above your bank account, your abilities, your understanding of how the world should work?

If you're honest, the answer is no. Not perfectly. Not consistently. Not even close. You trust your own judgment. You love your own comfort. You fear man's opinion more than God's. And this is not a minor failing — this is the *first* commandment, the foundation, the one from which all other sins flow. Every sin is, at root, a failure to fear, love, and trust God above all things. Including pornography. But not only pornography.

### The Second Commandment: "You shall not misuse the name of the Lord your God."

Luther explains: "We should fear and love God so that we do not curse, swear, use satanic arts, lie, or deceive by His name, but call upon it in every trouble, pray, praise, and give thanks."

When did you last take God's name lightly? Not just in crude profanity — that is the most obvious and least common form among Christians. What about casual, thoughtless invocations? What about praying without meaning it? What about invoking God's authority for your own opinions — "God told me" or "I feel led" when really you just wanted to do what you wanted to do? What about failing to call upon His name in trouble, choosing worry and self-reliance over prayer?

### The Fourth Commandment: "Honor your father and your mother."

Luther extends this to all authorities — parents, government, employers, pastors. Do you honor your pastor? Do you receive his preaching with gratitude and humility, or do you sit in the pew critiquing his sermon structure? Do you honor governing authorities, or do you grumble, mock, and refuse to respect the office even when you disagree with the officeholder? Do you honor your employer by working faithfully, or do you cut corners, waste time, and do the minimum?

### The Fifth Commandment: "You shall not murder."

Luther explains: "We should fear and love God so that we do not hurt or harm our neighbor in his body, but help and support him in every physical need."

You have never murdered anyone. Congratulations. But Christ said, "Everyone who is angry with his brother will be liable to judgment" (Matthew 5:22). Have you been angry? Have you nursed resentment? Have you harbored bitterness against someone who wronged you? Have you wished harm on someone — even quietly, even just in the privacy of your thoughts? Have you neglected to help your neighbor when you could have? Have you walked past need because it was inconvenient?

The man who thinks porn is his only problem has usually not spent much time examining his anger — the impatience with his children, the contempt for his coworkers, the cold fury at the driver who cut him off, the bitterness toward the family member who hurt him ten years ago. These are Fifth Commandment sins, and they are serious.

### The Eighth Commandment: "You shall not give false testimony against your neighbor."

Luther explains: "We should fear and love God so that we do not tell lies about our neighbor, betray him, slander him, or hurt his reputation, but defend him, speak well of him, and explain everything in the kindest way."

This one should stop every Christian man in his tracks. "Explain everything in the kindest way." When your coworker makes a mistake, do you explain it in the kindest way — or do you roll your eyes and tell someone else about it? When your wife does something that annoys you, do you explain it in the kindest way — or do you keep a mental ledger of her faults? When a public figure says something you disagree with, do you explain it in the kindest way — or do you assume the worst and broadcast your contempt?

Gossip. Slander. Uncharitable interpretation. Sarcasm deployed to diminish someone else. The failure to defend a person's reputation when others are tearing it down. These are Eighth Commandment sins, and most Christian men commit them daily without a flicker of conscience — because all their conscience is consumed by the one sin they've decided is the real problem.

### The Ninth and Tenth Commandments: "You shall not covet."

Luther explains that we should not scheme to get our neighbor's house, inheritance, wife, workers, or anything that belongs to him, but help him to keep what is his.

Coveting is not just wanting things. It is the deep, restless dissatisfaction with what God has given you — the conviction that you deserve more, that others have what should be yours, that God has been stingy with you. It is scrolling through social media and feeling the acid burn of envy at someone else's house, career, family, vacation, physique. It is comparing your wife to other men's wives, your salary to other men's salaries, your life to other men's lives.

This sin is epidemic among Christian men, and almost no one confesses it — because it doesn't *feel* dramatic enough to confess. It's just... the background radiation of the heart. The constant, low-grade grumbling that God hasn't given you enough.

## Why Porn Became "The Sin"

Here is what happened, and it is worth understanding.

Pornography use produces intense, immediate shame. The cycle of arousal, consumption, and disgust is so visceral, so undeniable, so obviously wrong, that it dominates the conscience. After a session, the shame is all you can feel. And because the shame is so loud, it drowns out everything else.

The man who looks at porn on Saturday night and then sits in church on Sunday morning is consumed by *that* guilt. He cannot hear the sermon because his conscience is screaming about what he did twelve hours ago. He prays about *that* sin. He resolves to fight *that* sin. He reads books about *that* sin. His entire spiritual life orbits around *that* sin like a planet around a sun.

And while all his attention is fixed on the one dramatic, visible, shameful sin, a dozen other sins sit comfortably in the dark, unexamined and unconfessed:

- The pride that makes him impatient with people he considers less intelligent.
- The anger he vents on his children when he's tired.
- The gossip he shares at work under the guise of "venting."
- The coveting that drives him to spend money he doesn't have on things he doesn't need.
- The laziness that keeps him from serving his neighbor when it's inconvenient.
- The self-righteousness that makes him silently judge other people's sins while agonizing over his own.
- The idolatry of comfort that keeps him from prayer, from Scripture, from worship, from sacrifice.

Pornography, for all its destructive power, was functioning as a *distraction* from a full reckoning with sin. The devil is perfectly happy to let you fixate on one sin — even to let you *conquer* that one sin — as long as you remain blind to all the others. A man who quits porn and then spends the rest of his life proud, angry, covetous, gossiping, and self-righteous is not a man who has been sanctified. He is a man who has rearranged the furniture in a burning house.

## The Pharisee's Prayer

Christ told the parable of the Pharisee and the Tax Collector for exactly this reason.

The Pharisee stood in the temple and prayed: "God, I thank you that I am not like other men, extortioners, unjust, adulterers, or even like this tax collector. I fast twice a week; I give tithes of all that I get" (Luke 18:11-12).

Notice: the Pharisee was not lying. He really did fast. He really did tithe. He really was not an extortioner or an adulterer. By the external metrics of religious performance, he was exemplary. His self-assessment was factually accurate.

And Jesus said the tax collector — the one who "would not even lift up his eyes to heaven, but beat his breast, saying, 'God, be merciful to me, a sinner!'" — went home justified, not the Pharisee.

Why? Because the Pharisee had reduced his sinfulness to a manageable checklist. He had identified the sins he was *not* committing, congratulated himself on them, and declared the project complete. He was blind to his pride, his contempt, his self-reliance, his fundamental failure to fear, love, and trust God above all things. He was, in his own estimation, basically fine — a good man with a clean record.

The Christian man who thinks porn is his only real problem is praying the Pharisee's prayer in reverse: "God, I confess that I am an adulterer — but at least I'm not angry, proud, covetous, lazy, gossiping, or idolatrous." It is the same error. The checklist is different, but the blindness is identical.

## What Freedom Reveals

Here is the uncomfortable truth that many men discover after they are set free from pornography:

When the porn is gone, the other sins become visible.

It is like living next to a construction site. While the jackhammers are running, you can't hear anything else. The noise fills the whole house. You think: "If only the construction would stop, my house would be so peaceful." And then the construction stops. And you realize you can now hear the leaky faucet, the creaking floor, the neighbor's dog, and the furnace that's been making that strange sound for months. The noise was masking a dozen other problems you didn't know you had.

Freedom from pornography does this. When the shame-fog lifts, you can finally see clearly — and what you see is not a basically good man who had one bad habit. What you see is a sinner. A real, thorough, comprehensive sinner who sins in thought, word, and deed, by what he has done and by what he has left undone, who has not loved God with his whole heart, and who has not loved his neighbor as himself.

This is not bad news. This is the prerequisite for the Gospel.

The man who thinks he has only one sin needs only a small Savior. A specialist. A divine consultant who handles one specific problem and then sends you on your way. But the man who knows he is a sinner from head to toe — who knows that his *best* works are stained with mixed motives, that his *most* righteous days are riddled with failures of love — that man needs what God is actually offering: total salvation. A Savior who doesn't patch the tire but replaces the whole car. A righteousness that is not his own, that comes from outside him, that covers *all* his sin — not just the dramatic, visible, shameful sin, but the quiet, respectable, hidden sin that nobody else sees.

"All have sinned and fall short of the glory of God, and are justified by his grace as a gift, through the redemption that is in Christ Jesus" (Romans 3:23-24). *All* have sinned. Not "all have committed one particular category of sin." All. In every category. Comprehensively.

## Simul Justus et Peccator — All the Way Down

The Lutheran confession *simul justus et peccator* — simultaneously righteous and sinner — is not just a statement about your status before God. It is a description of your daily experience.

You are righteous — completely, totally, without qualification — in Christ. His righteousness is credited to you. When God looks at you, He sees His Son. You are forgiven. You are holy. You are perfect in His sight — not because of anything you have done, but because of everything Christ has done.

And you are a sinner — completely, totally, without qualification — in yourself. Your old Adam is not merely damaged or weakened. He is corrupt to the core. He does not improve with age. He does not respond to self-help programs. He cannot be rehabilitated. He can only be drowned — daily, in the waters of baptism, through repentance and faith.

This means that quitting porn does not move you from the "sinner" column to the "righteous" column. You were already in the righteous column — by baptism, by grace, through faith. And you remain in the sinner column — because the old Adam is still there, still kicking, still producing sin in every category of the Commandments.

The man who quits porn and thinks he is now "basically sanctified" has misunderstood both the depth of his sin and the nature of his righteousness. His righteousness was never based on his behavior — it was always Christ's righteousness, given to him as a gift. And his sin was never limited to one category — it was always comprehensive, touching every corner of his life.

This is not cause for despair. It is cause for *deeper reliance on Christ.* The man who knows he is a sinner in ten categories, not just one, is a man who clings to the Gospel in all ten categories. He doesn't just repent of lust — he repents of pride, anger, coveting, gossip, laziness, self-righteousness, and the thousand daily failures of love that he used to ignore because porn was making too much noise.

And in every one of those categories, the same Gospel applies: "The blood of Jesus his Son cleanses us from all sin" (1 John 1:7). All sin. Not just the loud sin. Not just the dramatic sin. Not just the sexual sin. *All* sin.

## The Danger of the "Clean" Man

Let me be blunt about the practical danger here.

The man who quits porn and believes he has conquered his "main problem" is at risk of becoming insufferable. He may become:

- **Judgmental** — looking down on men who still struggle, forgetting that he was in the same trap not long ago, and ignoring the sins he commits daily that are no less offensive to God.
- **Self-righteous** — measuring his spiritual health by the absence of one sin rather than by the presence of love, humility, patience, and kindness.
- **Spiritually complacent** — assuming that because the big battle is won, the war is over. It is not over. It will not be over until the resurrection. The old Adam has a thousand weapons, and porn was only one of them.
- **Blind to his effect on others** — the man who has stopped looking at porn but still speaks harshly to his wife, still ignores his children, still gossips at work, still nurses resentment and bitterness, is a man whose household and community experience very little of the freedom he claims to have found.

Christ warned about this: "When the unclean spirit has gone out of a person, it passes through waterless places seeking rest, but finds none. Then it says, 'I will return to my house from which I came.' And when it comes, it finds the house empty, swept, and put in order. Then it goes and brings with it seven other spirits more evil than itself, and they enter and dwell there, and the last state of that person becomes worse than the first" (Matthew 12:43-45).

A house swept clean of one sin but filled with pride is not a free house. It is a house waiting to be reoccupied.

## What to Do About It

This is not a chapter designed to make you feel overwhelmed. "Great, so I'm failing at everything, not just the one thing? Thanks for the encouragement."

No. The point is this: you were never meant to fight sin alone, one commandment at a time, checking them off like items on a list. You were meant to be *carried* — by Christ, through the means of grace, in the fellowship of the Church.

**Examine yourself with the whole Catechism.** Before confession, don't just ask yourself whether you looked at porn. Walk through all Ten Commandments. Luther's "Christian Questions with Their Answers" in the Small Catechism is designed for exactly this. Let the Catechism do its diagnostic work on your *whole* life, not just the one area you've been fixated on.

**Confess specific sins.** Not just "I'm a sinner" in the abstract, and not just sexual sin. Confess the anger, the gossip, the coveting, the laziness, the pride. Name them. Bring them into the light. Hear the absolution cover *all* of them.

**Receive the Sacrament for all your sin.** When you kneel at the altar, you are not receiving the body and blood of Christ for your pornography problem. You are receiving it for your *whole* sinful self — for the pride and the anger and the coveting and the failures of love you didn't even notice. Christ's body was given for *you* — all of you, not just the part you're ashamed of.

**Pray the Lord's Prayer as a sinner, not as a conqueror.** "Forgive us our trespasses" — not "forgive me my trespass," singular. Every time you pray this prayer, you are confessing that you are a debtor in multiple categories, dependent on mercy across the board.

**Grow in the fruits of the Spirit.** The Christian life is not primarily about the *absence* of specific sins. It is about the *presence* of love, joy, peace, patience, kindness, goodness, faithfulness, gentleness, and self-control (Galatians 5:22-23). These are not achievements you produce — they are fruit that the Spirit grows in you as you remain connected to the Vine through Word and Sacrament. But they are the *real* measure of spiritual health, not the mere absence of one particular vice.

## A Prayer for Honest Self-Examination

Heavenly Father, I confess that I have been blind — not only to the sin that consumed my attention, but to the sins that hid in its shadow. I have been proud, and called it confidence. I have been angry, and called it righteous indignation. I have coveted, and called it ambition. I have gossiped, and called it honesty. I have been lazy in love, and told myself I was tired.

Forgive me. Not for one sin, but for all my sin — the sins I know, the sins I have ignored, and the sins I cannot even see. Create in me a clean heart, O God, and renew a right spirit within me.

Thank You that Your Son did not die for one category of sin but for *all* sin — that His blood cleanses completely, that His righteousness covers everything, that His grace is sufficient for a sinner as thorough as I am.

Keep me from the pride of the Pharisee — from thinking that my repentance has made me clean, from measuring myself against other men, from believing that I am basically fine now that the loud sin is quiet.

Drive me back to Your gifts — to the Word that diagnoses and heals, to the water that drowns the old Adam daily, to the table where Christ enters my sinful body and makes it holy, to the absolution that speaks Your verdict over my whole wretched, beloved life.

In the name of Jesus Christ, who came not for the righteous, but for sinners. Amen.
